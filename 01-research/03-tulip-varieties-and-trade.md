# Tulip Varieties and Trade: Historical Accuracy Assessment

> Research compiled May 2026 for Tulip Mania 1637 game project. This report assesses the historical accuracy of tulip varieties used in the game.

---

## 1. Tulip Varieties in the Game vs. History

### Variety Accuracy Assessment

| Game Variety | Historical Accuracy | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Semper Augustus** | ✅ ACCURATE | The most famous and expensive tulip. Red flames on white base. Existed and was THE premium variety. |
| **Viceroy** | ✅ ACCURATE | Second most famous variety. Well-documented in Pieter Cos 1637 catalog. Deep purple/violet flames. |
| **Gouda** | ⚠️ QUESTIONABLE | "Gouda" is NOT a well-documented tulip variety name from the 1630s. The city of Gouda was a tulip trading center, but no specific variety called "Gouda" appears in the major tulip catalogs. **Should be changed to a documented variety.** |
| **Black Tulip** | ❌ ANACHRONISTIC | No truly black tulip existed in 1637 — the first true black tulip was bred in **1986**, 350 years later. Dark tulips were deep purple or maroon (Couleren group). The "quest for a black tulip" became a legend via Dumas's 1850 novel. **Should be renamed to a dark variety that actually existed, or explicitly described as dark purple.** |

### Recommended Replacements

#### Instead of "Gouda"
Historically documented varieties that could replace it:
- **Admirael van der Eijck** — Well-documented in Pieter Cos catalog; sold for 1,045 guilders on Feb 5, 1637
- **Admirael Liefkens** — Premium variety
- **Generalissimo** — Premium variety (though less documented)
- **Paragon d'Utrecht** — Named after Utrecht
- **Groot Geel en Rooi van Leyde** — "Great Yellow and Red of Leiden"

#### Instead of "Black Tulip"
Historically documented dark varieties:
- **Prince van Astrijk** (Prince of Austria) — Dark variety
- **Zwarte Tulip** — While dark tulips existed, the specific "black" branding is anachronistic
- Simply describe it as a **"dark purple Couleren"** variety

## 2. The Semper Augustus — Full Historical Profile

### Description
- **Color**: Brilliant white base with vivid red "flames" or "feathers" streaking up the petals
- **Type**: Rosen group (white with red/pink flames)
- **Pattern**: Caused by Tulip Breaking Virus

### Price History

| Year | Price | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ~1623 | ~1,000 guilders | Early recorded price |
| ~1625 | ~1,200 guilders | Offered and refused |
| ~1633 | ~5,500 guilders | Reported value |
| 1637 peak | May have reached ~6,000+ guilders | At height of mania |
| Post-crash | ~10 guilders or less | >98% decline |

### Famous Owner: Adriaen Pauw
- **Grand Pensionary of Holland** (leading political figure)
- Owner of **Heemstede** estate near Haarlem
- Had a famous collection of Semper Augustus
- Reportedly **refused to sell** at any price — treated them as treasures, not commodities

### Why It Was So Expensive
1. The **Tulip Breaking Virus** caused the pattern but also weakened the plant
2. Each broken tulip was **unique** — patterns were unpredictable
3. The virus **reduced daughter bulb production** — supply was shrinking
4. Could NOT be reliably reproduced — growing from seed took 5-12 years and rarely "broke"
5. Extreme **scarcity** met extreme demand

## 3. The Viceroy — Full Historical Profile

### Description
- **Type**: Violetten or Bizarden group
- Deep purple/violet flames on lighter background
- Very large flower

### Famous Exchange
The Pieter Cos 1637 catalog records one Viceroy bulb exchanged for:

| Item | Quantity |
|---|---|
| Last of wheat | 4 |
| Last of rye | 8 |
| Fat cattle | 4 |
| Fat pigs | 8 |
| Fat sheep | 12 |
| Hogsheads of wine | 2 |
| Tons of butter | 4 |
| 1,000 lbs of cheese | 1 |
| Bed (complete) | 1 |
| Suit of clothes | 1 |
| Silver goblet | 1 |
| **Total value** | **~2,500-4,200 guilders** |

### Debate Over This Exchange
- **Mackay (1841)**: Presented this as an actual exchange
- **Krelage (1942) and Garber (2000)**: Argued this was a **rhetorical device** in a pamphlet to demonstrate the guilder's value, not a real transaction
- The truth likely lies somewhere between

## 4. The Admirael van der Eijck — Alternative for "Gouda"

### Description
- **Type**: Bizarden group — deep red/brown flames on yellow background
- **Well-documented**: Appears in Pieter Cos 1637 catalog
- **Sold for 1,045 guilders** on February 5, 1637
- One of the more expensive but not the most expensive varieties
- A "premium mid-tier" bulb — good substitute for "Gouda" in the game

## 5. The Biology of "Broken" Tulips

### The Tulip Breaking Virus

| Fact | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Cause** | Tulip Breaking Virus (TBV), a potyvirus |
| **Spread** | By aphids (plant lice) |
| **Effect on petals** | Creates multicolored, feathered, flamed patterns |
| **Effect on plant** | Progressive weakening; reduced daughter bulb production |
| **Discovery** | Not understood until the 1920s |
| **17th-century understanding** | Thought to be a desirable natural characteristic |

### Why This Mattered for Prices

1. **Unpredictability**: Each broken tulip was unique — you couldn't reproduce the exact pattern
2. **Rarity**: The virus reduced reproduction, so supply was inherently limited
3. **Deterioration**: Broken bulbs got weaker over time and eventually died
4. **No cure or control**: Growers couldn't induce "breaking" — it happened naturally (via aphids)
5. **Status**: Each broken tulip was a unique work of art created by nature

### Modern Status

- Broken tulips are **rare today** — the virus is controlled to protect commercial bulb crops
- Some heritage broken varieties are maintained in botanical gardens
- Modern "Rembrandt tulips" are bred to LOOK broken but are virus-free

## 6. The Tulip Trade Infrastructure

### Growing Season Calendar

| Month | Activity |
|---|---|
| **April-May** | Blooming season. Bulbs CANNOT be dug up or moved. Only paper trading. |
| **June** | Foliage dies back. Bulbs can be lifted and traded physically. |
| **July-August** | **Spot market** — physical bulbs traded. "Blank" market. |
| **September** | Bulbs must be replanted. |
| **October-November** | Bulbs planted in ground. Trading shifts to forward contracts. |
| **December-March** | **Peak speculation** — only forward contracts (windhandel). No physical delivery possible. |

### The "Tulip Letters" (Tulpenbrieven)

- Forward contracts for tulip bulbs
- Signed before a notary
- Specified variety, quantity, price, and delivery date
- Many were for bulbs still in the ground
- Not legally enforceable (treated as gambling debts)

### Key Trading Centers

| City | Role |
|---|---|
| **Haarlem** | Primary center of tulip growing and trading |
| **Amsterdam** | Major trading center, home of wealthy merchants |
| **Alkmaar** | Notable auctions (Orphan Chamber sale, Feb 5, 1637) |
| **Utrecht** | Had active Colleges |
| **Gouda** | Minor but real trading center |
| **Leiden** | Some trading activity |
| **Enkhuizen, Hoorn** | Minor trading |

### How Trading Actually Worked

1. **Spot trading** (summer): Physical exchange of bulbs
2. **Forward trading** (winter): Paper contracts for future delivery
3. **Auctions**: Organized events, sometimes for charity (Orphan Chamber)
4. **College trading**: Regular tavern-based trading sessions
5. **Individual deals**: Private transactions between known parties

## 7. Did People Really Sell Their Houses?

### The Historical Evidence

**Yes, but...**

- The Pieter Cos catalog and contemporary pamphlets describe single bulbs worth more than houses
- A Semper Augustus at 5,000-6,000 guilders was worth more than a luxury canal house (4,000-10,000 guilders)
- A Viceroy at 4,200 guilders was worth a canal house
- **However**: Goldgar found limited evidence of people ACTUALLY selling their houses for tulips
- The game character **Hendrik (sold his house for tulips)** is plausible as an individual case but should not be presented as typical

### The Famous "House for a Bulb" Claims

- Often based on pamphlet literature that was satirical/exaggerated
- Real transactions at these price levels existed but were rare
- Most trading was in paper contracts, not physical exchange of assets

## 8. Was Beer Really 2 Stuivers?

### Assessment

- **Yes, approximately** — 2 stuivers (0.1 guilders) per glass is a reasonable estimate
- This is the price used in the game's `historical-data.json`
- Beer was cheap and universally consumed
- A barrel of beer cost ~8-12 guilders
- The game's Maria character pricing beer at "two Stuivers a glass" is historically accurate
