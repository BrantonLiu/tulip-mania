## 1. The Windhandel (Wind Trade) System

### What Was Windhandel?

**Windhandel** literally means **"wind trade"** — a derogatory name for futures speculation where no physical goods ever changed hands. Traders bought and sold tulip bulbs they had never seen, often bulbs still in the ground that would not be delivered until the end of the growing season.

### How the Futures Market Worked

The tulip trade operated through a system of **forward contracts**:

- **Contract structure**: Buyers and sellers signed agreements before a notary to buy/sell specific tulip bulbs at a fixed price, with delivery at end of season.
- **Growing season constraints**: During bloom season (April-May), bulbs could NOT be dug up or moved. This created a natural window where only paper contracts could trade.
- **Spot market**: Physical bulbs could only be traded during summer months (June-September) when out of the ground. Known as the "blank" market (from "wissen" = to clean/empty).
- **No margin requirements**: Neither party paid initial margin or mark-to-market margin. All contracts were individual counter-party arrangements with no exchange or clearinghouse.
- **Settlement at maturity**: Profits and losses settled only when the contract came due, not daily.

### The "Wine Money" Fee (Wijnkoop)

Each trade incurred a **"wijnkoop" (wine money) fee** — a commission of approximately **2.5%** of transaction value, capped at maximum **3 guilders** per trade. Paid by the buyer, this effectively functioned as a transaction tax/commission.

### Wind Traders (Windhekken)

People who engaged in windhandel were called **wind traders** or **windhekken**:
- Had no intention of ever taking physical delivery of bulbs
- Had no expertise in horticulture
- Traded purely on price movements
- Often never even saw the bulbs they were trading
- Bought and sold the same contracts multiple times before delivery date

### Short Selling Bans

Dutch authorities had issued multiple edicts banning short selling (**"windverkoop"**):
- **1610**: First edict against short sales
- **1621**: Renewed ban
- **1630**: Renewed ban
- **1636**: Renewed ban (just before mania peaked)

However, enforcement was weak. Courts had consistently ruled that forward contracts were **unenforceable gambling debts**, paradoxically giving traders an escape hatch.

### NOT on the Official Exchange

Critical fact: tulip trading did **NOT** occur on the official Amsterdam Exchange (Beurs). It was considered beneath the dignity of formal commodities trading and took place entirely in taverns, on the margins of Dutch economic life.

## 2. Tavern Trading Culture

### The Tavern as Trading Floor

Tulip trading took place in **taverns and inns**, primarily in **Haarlem** and **Amsterdam**. These were not quiet, orderly venues — they were rowdy, wine-soaked gatherings.

### How Trading Sessions Worked

1. **Gathering**: Traders assembled at an appointed tavern, often in a back room
2. **Socializing**: Wine flowed freely. The "wine money" fee literally bought wine for the gathering
3. **Display**: A grower might bring a single prized bulb or a sketch/catalog showing varieties
4. **Bidding**: Prices called out and negotiated in a boisterous, auction-like atmosphere
5. **Recording**: A secretary recorded transactions in a ledger
6. **Binding ritual**: Deals sealed with handshake and drink

### The Atmosphere

- Extremely loud and chaotic — multiple negotiations simultaneously
- Fuelled by alcohol — wine and beer consumed heavily
- Social as much as economic — gatherings of like-minded enthusiasts
- Addictive — some participants neglected their businesses and families

### Seasonal Pattern

| Season | Activity |
|---|---|
| **Summer (June-Sept)** | Physical bulbs traded; the "blank" market |
| **Autumn (Oct-Nov)** | Bulbs planted; trading shifted to forward contracts |
| **Winter (Dec-March)** | Peak speculation; only paper contracts traded |
| **Spring (April-May)** | Blooming season; bulbs could not be disturbed |

The mania peaked during winter 1636-1637, when no physical trading was possible and speculation was most abstract.

## 3. The Colleges (Trading Clubs)

### What Were the Colleges?

The **"Colleges"** (also **"Collegie"** in Dutch) were organized trading clubs formed specifically for tulip speculation — the organizational backbone of the mania.

### Structure

- Each College had its own rules and customs
- Met at specific taverns on regular schedules (weekly or more frequently during peak)
- Members paid dues/fees (the wine money)
- A chairman/secretary was appointed to record transactions
- Membership by invitation or informal arrangement

### Meeting Format

1. Members gathered at designated tavern
2. A "plate" or slate was passed around for trading offers
3. Members wrote down bulbs they wanted to buy/sell and at what price
4. The secretary matched bids and asks
5. Transactions recorded in the College's ledger
6. Wine money collected on each transaction
7. Evening ended with socializing and drinking

### Geographic Distribution

- **Haarlem**: Center of tulip growing and trading; multiple active Colleges
- **Amsterdam**: Major trading center; attracted wealthy merchants
- **Utrecht**: Had its own Colleges
- **Alkmaar**: Smaller trading center
- **Gouda, Rotterdam, Hoorn, Enkhuizen**: Minor trading activity

## 4. Social Classes Involved

### The Popular Myth vs. Reality

Mackay (1841) portrayed tulip mania as affecting ALL levels of Dutch society. This has been **substantially challenged** by modern historians.

### What the Archives Show (Goldgar 2007)

- Trade was **"almost exclusively by merchants and skilled craftsmen who were wealthy, but not members of the nobility"**
- Participants were NOT common laborers or peasants
- Traders were part of a **tight-knit community** who knew each other personally
- Number of active traders: in the **hundreds**, not thousands

### Social Classes in the Trade

| Class | Role | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Nobility** | Some involvement as buyers of rare bulbs | Limited |
| **Wealthy Merchants** | Major speculators | Well documented in notarial records |
| **Skilled Craftsmen** | Active traders; many Mennonites | Goldgar's primary finding |
| **Growers/Horticulturists** | Producers; some became speculators | Core participants |
| **Tavern Keepers** | Hosts and informal brokers | Recorded in College minutes |
| **Common Laborers** | Minimal involvement | Minimal archival evidence |

### The Mennonite Connection

A disproportionate number of tulip traders were **Mennonites** — emphasizing community, mutual trust, and personal honor. This was critical because the entire system relied on trust (no margin, no enforcement). The Mennonite emphasis on keeping one's word provided the social infrastructure that made windhandel possible.

### The Honor System

Because forward contracts were legally unenforceable, the system depended on:
- **Personal honor**: Breaking a contract brought shame
- **Social pressure**: Everyone knew everyone
- **Community standing**: Default meant being shut out of future trading
- **Religious obligation**: For Mennonites and Calvinists, keeping one's word was a religious duty

This is why the crash was so devastating — it wasn't just financial ruin, it was a breakdown of social trust and community bonds.

## 5. The Crash Timeline: February 3-7, 1637

### Pre-Crash (Winter 1636-1637)

- Prices rising through autumn/winter 1636
- November 1636: particularly sharp increases
- January 1637: common bulb prices (by the pound/aas) began rising dramatically

### The Crash

#### Tuesday, February 3, 1637
- Auctioneer in **Haarlem** **failed to find buyers** for a routine auction lot
- First clear signal that demand had evaporated
- Panic began to spread

#### Wednesday-Friday, February 4-5, 1637
- February 5: **98 sales recorded** at wildly varying prices
- Alkmaar auction (Orphan Chamber charity) recorded high prices but may have been atypical
- Trading ground to a halt

#### Saturday, February 6, 1637
- Growers/traders in **Utrecht** elected representatives for Amsterdam assembly
- Panic spreading to all trading cities

#### Sunday, February 7, 1637
- Market effectively **collapsed completely**
- No buyers at any price for most varieties

### Speed of Collapse

- Prices went from peak to near-zero in **days**
- Transition from "cannot find buyer at current price" to "cannot find buyer at any price" happened in less than a week
- Many traders wealthy on paper Feb 1 were insolvent by Feb 7

## 6. Aftermath and Contract Resolution

### The Amsterdam Assembly (Late February 1637)
- Representatives from major trading cities gathered in Amsterdam
- Various settlement schemes proposed
- No agreement reached; assembly dissolved without binding resolution

### The Haarlem Solution (May 1637)
- Buyers could cancel contracts by paying **3.5% cancellation fee**
- Essentially a forced renegotiation favorable to buyers
- Contracts signed **before December 1636**: generally considered binding
- Contracts signed **after December 1636**: deemed cancellable

### Long-Term Aftermath

- Courts still busy with tulip disputes through **1639 and beyond**
- Many cases never fully resolved
- Tulip trade continued but at vastly reduced prices/volumes
- Episode became a cautionary tale in Dutch culture

### Economic Impact (Modern Assessment)

Despite the drama:
- Total value of tulip contracts at peak was a **fraction** of the Dutch economy
- Dutch Golden Age continued unabated
- VOC, banking sector, other industries unaffected
- No banks failed; no systemic financial crisis

## 7. Role of Government and Courts

### Dutch Government Response

- **No regulation** imposed on tulip trading during the bubble
- Existing short-selling bans (1610-1636) aimed at formal exchange, not tavern trading
- Tulip trading considered beneath formal economic governance

### The Court of Holland

- **Declined to rule** on substantive merits of tulip contracts
- Treated tulip contracts as **gambling debts** (weddenschappen)
- Under Dutch law, gambling debts were **not legally enforceable**

### February 24, 1637 Decree (Thompson's Theory)

- Self-regulating guild of Dutch florists announced all futures contracts written after Nov 30, 1636 were to be interpreted as **option contracts**
- Buyers relieved of obligation to buy; only required to compensate sellers with a small fixed percentage
- Thompson argues this explains the price patterns rationally

## 8. Specific Historical Figures

### Pieter Cos
- Tulip grower/merchant in Haarlem
- Published famous **1637 catalog** "Verzameling van een meenigte tulipaanen"
- One of the most important primary sources for tulip prices
- Recorded the **98 sales of February 5, 1637**

### Adriaen Pauw (1585-1653)
- **Grand Pensionary of Holland** (leading political figure)
- Owned estate of **Heemstede** near Haarlem
- Tulip enthusiast and collector
- His Semper Augustus bulbs were among the most famous in the Netherlands
- Reportedly refused to sell his Semper Augustus at any price

### Wouter Winkel (or Winckel)
- Merchant from Alkmaar whose death is connected to the tulip trade
- Auction of his collection in Alkmaar on February 5, 1637 (for orphaned children)
- One of the best-documented sales of the period

### Abraham de Goyer
- Amsterdam merchant and tulip grower
- One of the more prominent traders

### Jan van Goyen (1596-1656)
- Famous Dutch landscape painter
- Allegedly involved in tulip speculation and lost heavily (debated)

## 10. Scholarly Debate: Was It Really a Bubble?

### Traditional View (Mackay, 1841)
- Classic speculative bubble
- Affected all levels of Dutch society
- Prices detached from reality
- Widespread economic devastation

### Rational Market View (Peter Garber, 1989/2000)
- Rare bulbs were genuinely scarce (virus)
- High prices reflected real scarcity value
- Common bulb speculation was more bubble-like
- Overall economic impact was minimal

### Option Contract Theory (Earl Thompson, 2007)
- Feb 24, 1637 decree converted forwards to options
- High prices were rational option premiums
- The "bubble" was a rational response to government policy

### Social History View (Anne Goldgar, 2007)
- Trade limited to merchants and craftsmen, not whole population
- Social damage (broken trust) more significant than economic damage
- Popular narrative exaggerates both scale and impact

### Modern Consensus

1. Trade was **NOT** economy-wide; affected a limited community
2. **Economic impact** on Dutch Republic was minimal
3. Crash did **NOT** cause recession or financial crisis
4. **Social damage** within trading community was real and significant
5. Prices for rare bulbs had some justification (scarcity)
6. Prices for common bulbs by weight were clearly speculative
7. Episode tells us more about social networks and trust than economics
