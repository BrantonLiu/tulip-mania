## 1. Tavern Culture

### The Dutch Tavern (Herberg / Taveerne)

Taverns were central to Dutch social and economic life in the 1630s. They served as:
- **Trading floors** for tulip speculation (the "Colleges")
- **Social gathering places** for all classes
- **Information exchanges** where news and rumors spread
- **Accommodation** for travelers
- **Meeting places** for guilds and organizations

### What Taverns Looked Like

- Typically a ground-floor room with low ceilings
- Wooden tables and benches, sometimes with tablecloths for wealthier patrons
- A bar or counter where the keeper served drinks
- Walls often decorated with paintings (common in Dutch establishments)
- A hearth or fireplace for warmth in winter
- Sometimes a separate back room for private meetings (where Colleges met)
- Sign hung outside depicting the tavern's name (e.g., "The Golden Grape")

### Drinks Served

| Drink | Price | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Beer (bier)** | ~2 stuivers per glass | Most common drink; Dutch beer was famous across Europe |
| **Wine (wijn)** | More expensive | Imported from France, Rhineland, Spain |
| **Jenever (Dutch gin)** | Varies | Traditional Dutch spirit, juniper-flavored |
| **Brandewijn (brandy)** | Expensive | Distilled wine, a luxury |

### Beer in the 1630s

Beer was the standard drink for all classes — even children drank "small beer" (low-alcohol). The Netherlands was famous for its beer, and Amsterdam had dozens of breweries.

- **Typical beer price**: ~2 stuivers per glass (0.1 guilders)
- **A barrel of beer**: sold for ~8-12 guilders
- **Types**: Hemp beer (hennepbier), koyt beer, Jopen beer (stronger, exported)
- Amsterdam had ~70-100 breweries in the early 17th century

### Jenever (Genever / Dutch Gin)

- Existed since at least the 1500s (NOT invented by Franciscus Sylvius as sometimes claimed)
- Made from malt wine flavored with juniper berries
- Originally used for medicinal purposes
- Served in tulip-shaped glasses filled to the brim
- The "kopstootje" tradition: drink the first sip without holding the glass, bending over the table
- Amsterdam was a major jenever-producing city

### The Tavern Keeper's Role

The keeper was often a respected community figure who:
- Hosted gatherings and served as informal secretary
- Witnessed contracts (including tulip trades)
- Extended credit to regular customers
- Served as a source of news and information
- Maintained order during heated trading sessions

## 2. Currency and Purchasing Power

### The Guilder System

| Unit | Value |
|---|---|
| **1 Guilder (gulden/florijn)** | 20 stuivers |
| **1 Stuiver** | 16 penningen (or 8 duiten) |
| **1 Penning** | 2 duiten |

### Common Coins in Circulation

- **Guilder (gulden)**: Silver coin, the standard unit
- **Stuiver**: Silver coin, 1/20 of a guilder
- **Dubbele stuiver**: 2 stuivers
- **Schelling**: 6 stuivers
- **Rijksdaalder**: 2.5 guilders (silver)
- **Lion dollar (leeuwendaalder)**: ~2.4 guilders
- **Ducat**: ~3.5-4 guilders (gold)

| **Herring** | ~1-2 stuivers each | Common food |

## 3. Housing and Architecture

### The Amsterdam Canal Ring (Grachtengordel)

The famous canal ring was being built during the 1630s:
- **Construction began**: 1613 (Herengracht, Keizersgracht, Prinsengracht)
- **Major expansion**: 1660-1665 (the "Fourth Extension")
- In 1637, the canal ring was partially complete — many grand houses were already built

### Canal Houses (Grachtenpanden)

- **Narrow but deep** — property taxes were based on street frontage width
- **Typical width**: 6-7 meters (20-23 feet)
- **Typical depth**: 20-30 meters
- **Multiple stories**: 4-5 floors plus attic and basement
- **Distinctive features**:
  - Stepped or bell-shaped gable facing the street
  - A hoist beam (hijsbalk) at the top for lifting furniture
  - Large windows (the Dutch were famous for their glass)
  - Often a "voorhuis" (front house) and "achterhuis" (back house) with courtyard

### Interior Layout

- **Ground floor (bel-etage)**: Formal reception rooms, office
- **Upper floors**: Bedrooms, family living spaces
- **Basement (kelder)**: Kitchen, storage, servants' quarters
- **Attic (zolder)**: Storage, sometimes servants' quarters

### Heating

- **Tile stoves** (kachels) in some rooms
- **Fireplaces** in main rooms
- **Peat (turf)** was the primary fuel — the Netherlands had extensive peat bogs
- Coal was used but less common

### Common People's Housing

- **Workers and artisans**: Small houses, sometimes just 2-3 rooms
- **Many people rented** — Amsterdam had a large rental market
- **Almshouses (hofjes)**: Charitable housing for the elderly/poor

## 4. Women in Dutch Society

### Legal Status

Dutch women had **remarkably strong legal rights** for the era:

- **Widows** could inherit property and maintain control over finances and estates
- **Married women** had more limited rights under coverture, but Dutch law was more favorable than most European countries
- **Women could own property** — over 13,500 women owned property in Amsterdam between 1530-1811
- **Women could run businesses** — many shopkeepers and traders were women
- **Women took communion alongside men** — unusual in European churches

### Economic Participation

Dutch women were **more economically active** than their European counterparts:
- Many ran shops, inns, and taverns
- Women participated in markets and trade
- Widows often continued husbands' businesses
- Some women were involved in tulip trading

### Relevance to Game Characters

The character of **Anna (widow, 35)** is historically plausible:
- A widow inheriting tulip holdings from her husband is realistic
- A widow managing her own finances is realistic
- A widow being cautious about speculation is realistic
- Her concern for children's future is realistic

The character of **Maria (tavern keeper, 45)** is also plausible:
- Women running taverns was common
- A female tavern keeper would be well-informed about gossip and trade

## 5. Food and Drink

### Typical Dutch Diet in the 1630s

**Breakfast**: Bread with butter or cheese, sometimes porridge, beer
**Lunch**: Main meal — bread, fish or meat, vegetables, beer
**Dinner (evening)**: Lighter meal — bread, cheese, soup, beer

### Common Foods

| Category | Items |
|---|---|
| **Bread** | Rye bread (most common), wheat bread (more expensive) |
| **Dairy** | Butter, cheese (Edam, Gouda, Leiden) |
| **Fish** | Herring (national staple), cod, eel, sturgeon |
| **Meat** | Pork, beef, mutton, poultry (more expensive) |
| **Vegetables** | Cabbage, carrots, turnips, onions, peas, beans |
| **Fruit** | Apples, pears, cherries, berries |
| **Spices** | Pepper, cinnamon, nutmeg, cloves (available through VOC trade) |
| **Beverages** | Beer (universal), wine (wealthy), jenever, buttermilk |

### Dutch Herring

- The herring fishery was a cornerstone of the Dutch economy
- **Hollandse Nieuwe** (new herring) — first catch of the season, a delicacy
- Herring was salted and pickled for preservation
- Even the poor ate herring regularly

### The Spice Trade

- The VOC brought exotic spices from Asia
- Pepper, cinnamon, nutmeg, cloves became available
- Spices were still expensive but increasingly common in middle-class kitchens
- Amsterdam was Europe's spice trading capital

## 6. Clothing and Fashion

### General Styles

- **Dark, practical clothing** was the Dutch norm — a reflection of Calvinist values
- **Black** was extremely popular (and expensive to dye properly)
- **White ruffs/collars** (lieven) — the distinctive Dutch collar
- **Sumptuary laws**: Less strict than elsewhere; Dutch burghers dressed plainly by choice

### Men's Clothing

- Dark jacket (doublet/vest)
- Breeches (knee-length pants)
- White linen collar/ruff
- Stockings (often black or dark)
- Wide-brimmed hat
- Cloak in cold weather

### Women's Clothing

- Dark bodice/lacing
- Long skirt (often dark colored)
- White apron (for working women)
- Linen cap (muts) covering hair (married women)
- Jewelry for wealthy women (pearls popular)

### Social Distinction Through Clothing

- **Wealthy**: Finer fabrics (silk, fine wool), lace trim, gold/silver buttons, jewelry
- **Middle class**: Good quality wool, linen, neat appearance
- **Working class**: Coarser fabrics, simpler cuts, mended and repaired

## 7. Transportation

### Getting Around Amsterdam

- **Walking**: Primary mode for most people
- **Canal boats (trekschuit)**: Horse-drawn barges on canals — public transport
- **Rowboats**: For crossing canals and the Amstel river
- **Horse-drawn carriages**: For the wealthy
- **Horses**: For longer distances
- **Ships/vessels**: For inter-city travel (very common — Netherlands was water-based)

### Inter-City Travel

- **Regular barge service** connected all major Dutch cities
- Amsterdam to Haarlem: ~2-3 hours by barge
- Amsterdam to Leiden: ~4-5 hours
- Amsterdam to Utrecht: ~8-10 hours
- These barges ran on schedules and were the primary public transport

## 8. The VOC (Dutch East India Company) in Daily Life

### VOC's Presence in Amsterdam

- Founded **1602** with IPO raising 6.44 million guilders
- **Shares traded** on the Amsterdam Exchange — the world's first stock market
- **Dividend**: ~10-12.5% of face value per share annually
- **Face value of a share**: ~100 guilders
- Total value at peak: 78 million guilders

### How Ordinary People Related to VOC

- Many Amsterdam families had at least one member connected to maritime trade
- VOC shares could be bought and sold by anyone with capital
- VOC goods appeared in shops throughout the city
- VOC ships departing and arriving were public spectacles
- The VOC was a source of civic pride and wealth

### VOC and the Game

The game includes "航海股份" (Voyage Shares/VOC shares) as a tradeable asset:
- **Historically accurate**: VOC shares were actively traded
- **Price of ~100 guilders per share** is reasonable
- VOC shares provided a contrast to tulip speculation — they were "real" investments
- The VOC continued to thrive even after the tulip crash

## 9. Religious and Social Context

### Dutch Reformed Church

- The **dominant** church in the Dutch Republic
- **Calvinist** theology
- Attended by the majority but NOT compulsory — religious tolerance was a Dutch hallmark
- **Attitude toward speculation**: Mixed — Calvinism valued hard work and thrift, viewed gambling with suspicion
- **Attitude toward tulip mania**: Many ministers condemned it as gambling and vanity

### Religious Tolerance

- The Dutch Republic was famous for relative religious tolerance
- **Catholics, Jews, Lutherans, Mennonites, Remonstrants** all practiced (sometimes discreetly)
- Amsterdam had a significant **Jewish community** (Sephardic Jews from Portugal/Spain)
- **Mennonites** were overrepresented among tulip traders

### Social Customs

- **Handshake** sealed deals (important in tulip trading)
- **Drinking together** cemented business relationships
- **Dining** was communal and social
- **Church attendance** was Sunday norm (even if not strictly enforced)
- **Market days** were important social events

## 10. Key Historical Anachronisms to Watch in Game Dialogue

### Things That Should NOT Appear (anachronistic)

1. ❌ "Stock market crash" terminology — no such concept existed
2. ❌ "Investment portfolio" — modern concept
3. ❌ "Leverage" as a financial term — the concept existed but not the word
4. ❌ References to the "South Sea Bubble" (happened 1720, not yet known)
5. ❌ "Real estate agent" or "stockbroker" — these professions didn't exist

### Things That ARE Period-Appropriate

1. ✅ Forward contracts, windhandel, tulpenbrieven (tulip letters)
2. ✅ Guilder/stuivers/penningen monetary system
3. ✅ Beer, wine, jenever as tavern drinks
4. ✅ Canal houses, trekschuit barges, peat heating
5. ✅ Guild system, notaries, orphan chambers
6. ✅ VOC shares, dividends, the Exchange (Beurs)
7. ✅ Calvinist moral concerns about speculation
8. ✅ Social honor and trust as enforcement mechanism
