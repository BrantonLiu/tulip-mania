## 1. Primary Sources

### Contemporary (1630s-1640s)

#### Pieter Cos, "Verzameling van een meenigte tulipaanen" (1637)
- **What it is**: A catalog of tulip varieties with watercolor illustrations and recorded prices
- **Published**: Haarlem, 1637
- **Significance**: The most important primary source for tulip prices at the peak
- **Contains**: The famous "98 sales of February 5, 1637"
- **Available**: Digitized at Wageningen UR Library

#### "Samen-spraeck tusschen Waermondt ende Gaergoedt" (1637)
- **What it is**: Three anonymous dialogues about the tulip trade
- **Published**: By Adriaen Roman, Haarlem, February-May 1637
- **Characters**: Waermondt ("True-mouth") and Gaergoedt ("Greedy-goods")
- **Significance**: Primary source for popular reactions and trading culture
- **Referenced by**: Anne Goldgar extensively in her 2007 book

#### "Flora's Mallewagen" (Flora's Wagon of Fools) (c.1637)
- **What it is**: Allegorical painting by Hendrik Gerritsz Pot
- **Content**: Goddess Flora on a fool's cart loaded with tulip bulbs
- **Significance**: Visual satire of the mania; draws on "Ship of Fools" tradition

#### Notarial Archives
- **Cities**: Haarlem, Amsterdam, Utrecht, Alkmaar
- **What they contain**: Forward contracts, dispute records, estate inventories
- **Used by**: Goldgar (2007) for her comprehensive study
- **Significance**: Show real transactions, not just pamphlet rhetoric

#### Orphan Chamber (Weeskamer) Records
- **Alkmaar auction of February 5, 1637**: Well-documented sale for charity
- **Significance**: Provides verified price data

#### Court Records (1637-1639+)
- **Courts of Holland, Haarlem, Amsterdam**
- Dispute cases over tulip contracts
- Courts still hearing tulip cases years after the crash

### Near-Contemporary (17th-18th Century)

#### Joseph de la Vega, "Confusión de Confusiones" (1688)
- **What it is**: Book about the Amsterdam Stock Exchange
- **Language**: Spanish (written by a Portuguese-Jewish merchant in Amsterdam)
- **Coverage**: Primarily about stock trading, not tulips
- **Significance**: Describes Dutch financial culture 50 years after tulip mania
- **Relevance**: Shows how the Dutch financial system evolved

## 2. Charles Mackay's Account (1841) — The Source of the Myth

### "Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds" (1841)

#### What Mackay Wrote
- Dedicated a chapter ("The Tulipomania") to the Dutch tulip bubble
- Portrayed it as affecting **all levels of Dutch society**
- Claimed widespread economic devastation
- Included famous anecdotes:
  - A sailor mistakenly eating a valuable tulip bulb
  - People selling their homes for single bulbs
  - Universal participation from nobles to chimney sweeps
  - Dramatic suicides and ruined families

#### How Accurate Is Mackay?

**Short answer: Largely unreliable as historical source.**

| Mackay's Claim | Modern Assessment |
|---|---|
| "All levels of society participated" | ❌ **False** — Goldgar (2007) showed trade was limited to merchants and craftsmen |
| "Widespread economic devastation" | ❌ **False** — Economic impact was minimal; Dutch Golden Age continued |
| "People sold homes for bulbs" | ⚠️ **Exaggerated** — Some high prices recorded but mass property sales not documented |
| "Universal speculation" | ❌ **False** — Hundreds of traders, not the whole population |
| "Suicides and ruin" | ⚠️ **Exaggerated** — Goldgar found fewer than half a dozen financial troubles clearly linked to tulips |

#### Why Mackay Got It Wrong

1. **Relied on pamphlet literature** without critical assessment — pamphlets were satirical, not journalistic
2. **Wrote 200+ years after events** — no access to archives
3. **Victorian moralizing** — his book was meant as moral instruction, not history
4. **No peer review** — 19th-century "history" lacked modern scholarly standards
5. **Confirmation bias** — selected dramatic anecdotes that fit his thesis

#### Mackay's Enduring Influence

Despite its inaccuracies, Mackay's account:
- Has been **continuously in print** since 1841
- Shaped **every popular account** of tulip mania for 180+ years
- Was cited during the **dot-com bubble** (1995-2001) and **2008 financial crisis**
- Was quoted by the Dutch Central Bank president about Bitcoin (2013)

## 3. Modern Scholarly Reassessment

### Peter Garber (1989, 2000) — The Rational Markets View

#### "Tulipmania" (Journal of Political Economy, 1989)
#### "Famous First Bubbles: The Fundamentals of Early Manias" (MIT Press, 2000)

**Core Arguments:**
- Rare bulb prices (Semper Augustus, Viceroy) reflected **genuine scarcity**
- The Tulip Breaking Virus made rare bulbs truly rare and getting rarer
- **Common bulb** speculation was more bubble-like but involved relatively small sums
- Similar price patterns in other rare flowers (hyacinths in 18th century)
- A small quantity of prototype lily bulbs sold for 1 million guilders in modern times
- The overall economic impact was **minimal**

**Significance**: First serious academic challenge to the Mackay narrative from an economist.

---

### Earl Thompson (2007) — The Option Contract Theory

#### "The Tulipmania: Fact or Artifact?" (Public Choice, 2007)

**Core Arguments:**
- The February 24, 1637 decree **converted forward contracts to option contracts**
- Buyers were given the right (not obligation) to complete purchases
- If traders anticipated this conversion, high prices were **rational** — they were buying options, not obligations
- The observed price patterns are consistent with **option pricing theory**
- The annualized rate of price decline was 99.999% — far faster than typical flower price declines
- The "bubble" was actually a **rational response to government policy**

**Significance**: Provides an elegant economic explanation that doesn't require "mass irrationality."

---

### Anne Goldgar (2007) — The Social History Revolution

#### "Tulipmania: Money, Honor, and Knowledge in the Dutch Golden Age" (University of Chicago Press, 2007)

**The definitive modern work.** Goldgar, a historian at King's College London, spent years in Dutch archives.

**Core Findings:**

1. **Limited participation**: Trade was "almost exclusively by merchants and skilled craftsmen who were wealthy, but not members of the nobility"
2. **Not economy-wide**: The number of active traders was in the hundreds
3. **Minimal economic damage**: "Fewer than half a dozen" people experienced financial troubles clearly linked to tulips
4. **Social damage was real**: Friendships destroyed, community bonds broken, trust networks shattered
5. **Mennonite connection**: Disproportionate number of traders were Mennonites — their trust-based community enabled the trade
6. **Pamphlets were propaganda**: Written by religious moralists, not victims
7. **The popular narrative is mostly wrong**: "As Anne Goldgar reveals in Tulipmania, not one of these stories is true"

**Key Quote:**
> "Even though the financial crisis affected very few, the shock of tulipmania was considerable. A whole network of values was thrown into doubt."

**Significance**: Fundamentally reshaped academic understanding. The book won major awards and is the standard scholarly reference.

## 4. What the Game Gets WRONG (Based on Current Scholarship)

### Critical Issues

| Game Element | Issue | Scholarly Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Widespread ruin** | Game shows multiple NPCs ruined; in reality, very few were | Goldgar found <6 documented cases of tulip-related financial trouble |
| **Common people participating** | Game implies everyone was involved | Trade was limited to merchants and skilled craftsmen |
| **"Selling the house" (Hendrik)** | Possible but not typical | Goldgar found limited evidence of property sales for tulips |
| **Complete devastation** | Game's ending implies total economic ruin | Dutch economy was unaffected; Golden Age continued for decades |

### Nuanced Assessment

The game can be both **entertaining** and **historically responsible** by:
1. Acknowledging the exaggeration in its narrative
2. Including Maria's skeptical voice (which aligns with nuchterheid)
3. Showing that the "ruin" was more social than economic
4. Having the Stranger character reference the limited scope of the damage

### What the Game Gets RIGHT

| Element | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Tavern-based trading | ✅ Correct |
| Forward contracts (windhandel) | ✅ Correct |
| Honor-based enforcement | ✅ Correct (Goldgar's key finding) |
| Social destruction of trust | ✅ Correct (more important than financial loss) |
| Rapid crash in February 1637 | ✅ Correct |
| Limited government intervention | ✅ Correct |
| Mennonite/Calvinist moral concerns | ✅ Correct |
| Beer prices, guilder values | ✅ Approximately correct |
| Anna as cautious widow | ✅ Culturally accurate |
| Maria as voice of reason | ✅ Embodies Dutch nuchterheid |
